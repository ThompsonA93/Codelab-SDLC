## Conceptualization of Ideas

The conceptualization of ideas can be best broken down into two types of thinking - Divergent vs. Convergent: 

Originally formalized by psychologist J.P. Guilford (ca. 1967), Divergent thinking expands the problem space to generate a high volume of diverse, unique ideas, while Convergent thinking applies logic, constraints, and criteria to filter, refine, and select the single best direction.


### Ideation

- Crazy 8s: A fast-paced sketching method popularized by Google Ventures Design Sprint framework. The goal is to sketch eight distinct ideas in eight minutes.
- Storyboards: Taken from cinematography, storyboards visually map a user's journey or interaction over time, highlighting context and state.
- Mindmaps: Popularized by Tony Buzan (ca. 1974), the designer conceptualizes the core concept with sub-concepts which recursively branch and associate.
- Bodystorming: Physically role-play spaces, interactions or user-interactions.

### Anti-Patterns

- Reverse Brainstorming: The designer looks for ideas that cause a problem or worsen it rather than solve it. These are then inverted to reveal overlooked solutions and failure points.
- Dark Horse Prototyping: The designer deliberately pursues a high-risk, a radical, or a wild card concept that was previously discarded or ignored. 
- Assumption Smashing: The creation of scenarios where the designer systemically creates assumptions that violate or inverte structural assumptions specifically on a product or a problem space.



## Prototyping Methodologies

### Low/Mid/High-fidelity Prototypes

- Low-fidelity represent low-cost prototypes used in early pitches to validate core concepts, workflows and information hierarchy without the specifics.
- Mid-fidelity introduce accurate layouts and basic navigation, without branding, colours or styling.
- High-fidelity prototypes closely mimic the final product. Often used for usability testing and stakeholder pitches.

### Horizontal/Vertical Prototyping

- Vertical Prototyping views the depth of a specific use-case. It cuts through database and APIs, logic workflows within the application, or user views.
- Horizontal prototyping views the breadth of a specific use-case. Exemplary, it views a wide range of user interfaces for interaction, but contains no major aspects on functionality or backend integration.

## Realization of Prototypes

### Paper vs. Digital Prototypes

- Paper prototypes showcase interfaces via sketches or even cutouts. The pitcher acts as a computer by swapping paper elements based on where a tester clicks.
- Digital prototypes are created using software tools to simulate interactions, transictions and user stories on screen.s

### Throwaway vs. Evolutionary Prototypes

- Throwaway prototypes are created for a single iteration of the product, and commonly used for learning about the specific workflows.
- Evolutionary prototypes are continuously developed and serve as minimum viable product showcase.


## Prototype Analytics & Tuning

### SCAMPER

A checklist developed by Bob Eberle (1971) to systematically modify a prototype.

- Substitute: What materials, steps or features can be replaced?
- Combine: Can this be merged with another step or feature?
- Adapt: What context can this be adjusted to, to fit in?
- Modify: Can we change the scale, color or shape?
- Put to another use: How would this serve a different feature?
- Eliminate: What can be streamliend or removed?
- Reverse: What happens if we flip the user sequence or layout?

### DFV Framework

Popularized by global design firm IDEO, 2008.

- Desirability: Does the product solve a real human need and do people want it?
- Feasability: Is it functionally and technologically possible to build it?
- Viability: Does it make long-term financial sense to form a sustainable business model around it?
